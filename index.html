Biography

Andy Weir built a two-decade career as a software engineer until the success of his first published novel, The Martian, allowed him to live out his dream of writing full-time. He is a lifelong space nerd and a devoted hobbyist of subjects such as relativistic physics, orbital mechanics, and the history of manned spaceflight. He also mixes a mean cocktail. He lives in California.

Born June 16, 1972, in Davis, California, and raised in Livermore, Weir grew up in a household of engineers — his father was a physicist at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, and his mother was an electrical engineer. He was hired as a computer programmer at the age of fifteen, and later studied computer science at UC San Diego before leaving to pursue software engineering roles at companies including AOL, Palm, MobileIron, and Blizzard.

Weir spent years writing science fiction for free on his personal website. The Martian began as a serialized story there — researched down to the exact orbital mechanics, Martian weather, and botany required to strand astronaut Mark Watney believably on the Red Planet. Fans asked for a Kindle version; Weir uploaded it for 99 cents, the story climbed the Kindle bestseller charts, and a literary agent brought it to Crown Publishing Group. The rest is orbital history.

His writing philosophy is simple: the science has to work. Every survival trick, every equation, every improvised repair is grounded in real engineering — problem-solving under pressure, delivered with the dry, self-deprecating humor of an engineer narrating his own near-death experience. That same approach carried through Artemis's lunar-gravity heist and Project Hail Mary's astrobiology and first-contact puzzle-solving.

TECHNICAL BRIEFING

THE SCIENCE BEHIND THE FICTION

CALCULATIONS VERIFIED

Every transfer window, launch trajectory, and rendezvous burn in Weir's novels is worked out with real orbital mechanics — the same math NASA uses to plot Hohmann transfers between Earth and Mars. In The Martian, the timing of Mark Watney's resupply and rescue windows is dictated by where Earth and Mars actually sit in their orbits, not narrative convenience.

Project Hail Mary builds an entire ecology around a fictional extremophile organism that feeds on stellar energy, then works out its biochemistry, reproduction, and vulnerabilities with the rigor of a research paper. The novel's first-contact biology is grounded in real questions about how life might function under radically different conditions.

Artemis works through the practical engineering of a permanent lunar settlement: pressurized domes, welding in one-sixth gravity, oxygen economics, and the industrial processes — like aluminum smelting — that could realistically sustain a city on the Moon.
