SOL 0002 · THE AUTHOR

Two decades in engineering. A lifetime obsessed with space.

Andy Weir spent nearly twenty years working as a software engineer — including a stint at Blizzard Entertainment — before the runaway success of his debut novel let him write full time. He grew up in Livermore, California, the son of a physicist and an electrical engineer, and started programming professionally at fifteen.

A self-described lifelong space nerd, Weir treats relativistic physics, orbital mechanics, and the history of manned spaceflight as hobbies rather than homework. That obsession with getting the numbers right is what turned The Martian — a novel he originally serialized for free on his own website — into a 99-cent Kindle bestseller, then a Crown Publishing acquisition, then a No. 12 New York Times bestseller, then a Ridley Scott film.

"Complex science, explained in an entertaining and accessible way."

That philosophy carried through Artemis, a heist thriller set in humanity's first lunar city, and Project Hail Mary, a first-contact survival story that became a second commercial phenomenon and a 2026 feature film starring Ryan Gosling. Weir received the John W. Campbell Award for Best New Writer in 2016, and Project Hail Mary was a finalist for the 2022 Hugo Award for Best Novel.

The Martian

Six days into a Mars mission, astronaut Mark Watney is left for dead after a dust storm forces his crew to evacuate without him. Alone on a hostile planet with failing supplies, he has to engineer his own survival — one problem, one experiment, one wisecrack at a time.

The novel began as a free serial on Weir's own website before reader demand pushed it to Kindle, where it climbed the bestseller charts on word of mouth alone. It later debuted on the New York Times bestseller list and was adapted into the 2015 film directed by Ridley Scott.

Artemis

Jazz Bashara is a smuggler scraping by in Artemis, humanity's first and only lunar city, when a shot at a life-changing score pulls her into a full-blown conspiracy for control of the colony itself.

Weir builds Artemis with the same engineering rigor he applied to Mars — economics, life support, and lunar law all get worked out in detail — while keeping the plot moving with a wisecracking heroine, a ticking clock, and no shortage of large explosions.

Project Hail Mary

Ryland Grace wakes up alone on a spacecraft millions of miles from home with no memory of his mission, his crewmates dead beside him, and humanity's survival resting entirely on whether he can piece it all back together in time.

A #1 New York Times bestseller and 2022 Hugo Award finalist, praised by critics for pairing genuine scientific problem-solving with real warmth. The 2026 film adaptation stars Ryan Gosling and Sandra Hüller, directed by Phil Lord and Christopher Miller.

Cheshire Crossing

Alice, Wendy, and Dorothy — now teenagers who've had enough of meddling authority figures — reunite at a boarding school for girls who've crossed into magical worlds, and immediately go looking for trouble across the multiverse.

Originating as Weir's own fan fiction, the story was reimagined as a graphic novel with illustrator Sarah Andersen (Sarah's Scribbles), earning a starred Kirkus review for its blend of humor and inventive world-crossing.
